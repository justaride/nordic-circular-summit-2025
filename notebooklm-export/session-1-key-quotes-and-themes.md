## üéØ CORE THEMES

### **1. VALUE REDEFINITION**

**Nature as Primary Value**
- Circular economy measured in kilos (material flows) not currency
- Balance between human and nature values currently imbalanced
- Nature value becoming scarce, driving circular economy importance
- Moving from meaningless growth to sustainable development

**Quote:**
> "Value is the core essence of us. It's the value of our life. It's a nature value that we belong to, and it's really the balance between human and nature values that is now in imbalance of the old systems."
> ‚Äî Einar Kleppe Holthe

---

### **2. TRUST & COLLABORATION**

**Nordic Cultural Assets**
- Trust as foundational infrastructure (especially in Arctic contexts)
- Collaboration, design thinking, technology as Nordic strengths
- Pre-competitive frameworks enabling business collaboration
- Sense of responsibility remaining strong in Nordic societies

**Examples:**
- Denmark's Circular Furniture Network: 30 competing brands collaborating
- Voluntary Sector Collaboration on Textiles
- Nordic Circular Investments project (Finland, Sweden, Norway)

**Quote:**
> "Competing businesses sit together in these networks and they collaborate on challenges that are too big for the individual company to face on their own."
> ‚Äî Frederik Thrane

---

### **3. ADAPTABILITY OVER SCALE**

**Rethinking Growth Models**
- Adaptable models more appropriate than rigid scalable models
- Context matters: rural vs urban, local vs global
- Arctic strengths: reuse, resource respect, local self-reliance
- 28 million Nordic market as platform, not template

**Quote:**
> "Talking about scalable models, business models would be a tad optimistic. So we would say we understand scalable models as **adaptable models**."
> ‚Äî Edvard Lybert M√∏rk

---

### **4. POLITICAL GAP**

**Ambition vs. Implementation**
- Paper commitments to sustainability vs. short-term economic focus
- Circular strategies broad, non-binding, politically fragile
- EU leadership (Circular Economy Commissioner) vs. national lag
- Need for alignment between national politics and EU targets

**Denmark Exception:**
- National action plan on textiles in Fiscal Act
- Government prioritizing previously neglected sectors
- Movement from rhetoric to resource allocation

**Quote:**
> "Last year, there was a decision to include a national action plan on textiles in the Danish Fiscal Act. And this plan is focused on reduction. And that is pretty historical."
> ‚Äî Frederik Thrane

---

### **5. INVESTMENT BARRIERS**

**Why Circular Is "Too Risky"**
- Longer timelines required for circular business models
- Physical infrastructure needs (factories, logistics)
- Market education necessary
- Multi-stakeholder collaboration networks take time to build

**Solutions Needed:**
- Hybrid public-private investment models
- KPIs for measuring circular impact
- Best practice libraries
- Lower risk through collaborative funding

**Quote:**
> "For many years, circular economy has been seen as something that is not so investable, related to the risk. Circular solutions require a factory or other physical aspects that require more time."
> ‚Äî Alisa Mick

---

### **6. YOUTH & SYSTEM CHANGE**

**Three Levels of Circular Economy**
1. **Micro**: Individual/company practices
2. **Economy-wide**: Percentage circular (currently 4% globally)
3. **System**: Macroeconomic design, incentives, decision-making

**Youth Strategies:**
- **Outside pressure**: Direct action (e.g., NaturUngdom Norway blocking oil field)
- **Inside participation**: Advisory boards (e.g., Generation 2030 Finland)
- 18 organizations in Regeneration 2030 network across Nordic-Baltic region

**Demands:**
1. Invite organized youth groups into decision rooms
2. Support street activism
3. Don't criminalize climate action

## üé® METAPHORS & FRAMEWORKS

### **The Fish Don't Know What Water Is**
Used to describe how we're embedded in a 250-year-old linear economy without recognizing it. We need to step outside to see the system.

### **Trust as Infrastructure**
Reframes infrastructure from physical (roads, buildings) to social (relationships, reliability). Particularly relevant in Arctic contexts.

### **Turning the Iceberg Upside Down**
Using geographic challenges (remoteness, harsh climate) as advantages for symbiotic circular models.

### **Symbiosis as Superpower**
Nature's waste-free ecosystem model applied to industrial organization.

### **Sandbox to Testbed to Scale**
Progressive model: Baltics test ‚Üí Nordics validate ‚Üí Global scale

### **Kilos Not Krona**
Circular economy measured by material flows (physical reality) not financial abstractions.

## üí° ACTIONABLE INSIGHTS

### **For Policymakers**
1. Align national politics with EU circular economy targets
2. Move circular strategies from non-binding to fiscal commitments
3. Include previously neglected sectors (textiles, furniture)
4. Invite organized youth groups into decision-making
5. Support both inside and outside pressure for change

### **For Investors**
1. Develop hybrid public-private investment models
2. Accept longer timelines for circular business development
3. Use KPIs for circular impact measurement
4. Build best practice libraries
5. Understand that circular requires physical infrastructure and collaboration

### **For Businesses**
1. Engage in pre-competitive collaboration frameworks
2. Unite supply chains, not just individual companies
3. Develop scalable repair systems
4. Build adaptable (not just scalable) models
5. Participate in transition groups (textiles, construction, etc.)

### **For Regions & Cities**
1. Organize for industrial and regional symbiosis
2. Connect rural and urban value chains
3. Build trust as foundational infrastructure
4. Use geographic challenges as advantages
5. Focus on local value creation, global connections

### **For Knowledge Institutions**
1. Reskill for circular economy thinking
2. Help "unlearn" 250 years of linear assumptions
3. Build human and knowledge infrastructure
4. Teach system-level thinking, not just micro practices
5. Connect traditional knowledge with modern innovation
